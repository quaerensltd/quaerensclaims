Common problem

Were you sold a dream that became a financial burden?

Many buyers are encouraged to purchase a caravan, lodge or holiday home after being told it could generate income, hold its value, or be easy to sell later.

Problems often appear after the sale, when site fees rise, rental income falls short, finance becomes difficult, or the park will not allow a straightforward exit.

You may have grounds for a review if:

  • • You were told it was a good investment
  • • Rental income or resale value was exaggerated
  • • Fees and annual increases were not made clear
  • • You felt pressured to sign on the day
  • • Finance terms or affordability were not properly explained
  • • You later discovered restrictions on selling, subletting or exiting

Common mis-selling practices

  • Promises of rental income or guaranteed returns — income projections may have been exaggerated or presented as more certain than they were.
  • Misleading information about site fees or annual increases — ownership may have become unaffordable once full costs were understood.
  • Limited or no resale options — buyers may have been told resale was simple, then later discovered restrictions, commissions or poor buy-back values.
  • Contracts with unfair terms or hidden clauses — important conditions may not have been explained clearly before signing.
  • Pressure sales techniques during site visits — some buyers report being rushed, emotionally pressured or pushed into same-day decisions.
  • Finance agreements that were not properly explained — credit terms, affordability, interest or commission arrangements may not have been made clear.

If any of these sound familiar, your case may be worth checking in more detail.

What are your rights?

UK consumer law may protect you where a purchase was influenced by misleading information, aggressive sales tactics or unclear contract terms.

  • Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 — protects consumers from misleading actions, misleading omissions and aggressive commercial practices.
  • Consumer Rights Act 2015 — supports fair treatment, clear terms and remedies where goods or services are not as described.
  • Misrepresentation Act 1967 — may apply where false or misleading statements influenced your decision to enter the agreement.

The outcome depends on the evidence, what was said before purchase, the documents you signed, and the losses you suffered.
