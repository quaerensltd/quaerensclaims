Situation

Your landlord has a duty to keep your home safe and in repair

Housing disrepair occurs when a rented property falls below acceptable living standards because necessary repairs have not been carried out. This can include damp, mould, leaks, broken heating, unsafe electrics, pest infestations, damaged ceilings, or other serious issues.

If you have reported the problem and it has not been fixed within a reasonable time, it may be worth reviewing whether action can be taken to secure repairs and claim financial redress.

Common disrepair issues include:

  • • Damp, mould, leaks, or water ingress
  • • Broken heating or hot water systems
  • • Faulty electrics, lighting, or unsafe wiring
  • • Cracked walls, unsafe floors, or structural damage
  • • Pest infestations, blocked drains, or sewage issues

Could this apply to you?

You may qualify if:

  • You reported the issue and it was not fixed
    Written complaints, emails, messages, and repair requests can help show the landlord was aware.
  • Your daily life or health has been affected
    Damp, mould, unsafe electrics, or lack of heating can cause significant disruption and risk.
  • You have evidence of the problem
    Photos, videos, inspection reports, medical letters, repair logs, and damaged belongings can all help.
  • You have suffered loss or inconvenience
    This may include damaged items, extra costs, rooms being unusable, or prolonged poor living conditions.

Tenant rights

What landlords are usually expected to maintain

Structure and exterior

Roofs, walls, ceilings, windows, stairs, and floors should not present safety risks or serious deterioration.

Heating, plumbing and sanitation

Tenants should have working heating, hot water, plumbing, drainage, and sanitation installations.

Electrical and safety hazards

Faulty wiring, unsafe sockets, fire risks, pest infestations, and health hazards should be addressed properly.
