Key issue

What is cross-border insolvency or recovery?

Local company insolvent

A business in one country may enter liquidation, administration, bankruptcy, or another insolvency process, leaving creditors unpaid.

Parent company still active

Sometimes the wider group, parent company, or related brand continues trading elsewhere while the local entity claims no funds remain.

Assets or operations abroad

Assets, contracts, payment flows, or management decisions may sit in another jurisdiction and need to be assessed carefully.

Creditors, consumers, and investors are often told there is nothing left to recover. That may be true in some cases, but not always. The key is to review the structure, control, contracts, payment records, and conduct before deciding whether the matter is closed.

Who may be affected?

Cross-border problems affect both businesses and consumers

Creditors of international groups

Businesses owed money by a company connected to overseas entities may need to assess whether group conduct or assets matter.

Consumers left after collapse

Consumers who paid deposits, fees, or subscriptions to a business that suddenly shut down may still need a proper review.

Investors in overseas schemes

Foreign property, finance, investment, and timeshare structures may involve parties in more than one country.

Unclear jurisdiction disputes

Contracts may refer to another country, but that does not always mean there are no practical complaint or recovery options.

Possible rights and routes

What legal or recovery issues may be relevant?

EU insolvency cooperation

Regulation frameworks may support cooperation between courts and insolvency practitioners where EU assets or creditors are involved.

Corporate structure abuse

In certain cases, courts may examine whether a company structure has been used unfairly or abusively to avoid obligations.

Fraudulent or wrongful trading

Directors or officers may face scrutiny where a company traded while insolvent or where creditors were misled.

Group liability and consumer law

Shared branding, control, common representations, or coordinated conduct may be relevant in some consumer and creditor cases.

The correct route depends on the country, contract, evidence, company structure, and timeline. This page is general information only and is not legal advice.

Example scenarios

Cases that may deserve review

Foreign property firm collapse

A buyer or investor paid money into an overseas property project, but the local company dissolved or stopped responding.

Subsidiary closes, parent trades on

A local company claims it cannot pay creditors, while a parent or related business continues using the same operation or brand elsewhere.

Brand continues after liquidation

A company is liquidated in one country, but very similar branding, websites, staff, or services continue in another jurisdiction.

International unpaid debt

Goods or services were delivered across borders, but the overseas party failed to pay and jurisdiction is unclear.

Failed cross-border service

A service was sold internationally, but what was delivered did not match the promise or contract.

Consumer or investor misled abroad

Marketing, contracts, payment requests, or guarantees may have been misleading or incomplete.
