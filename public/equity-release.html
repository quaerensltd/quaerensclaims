What Are Equity Release Issues?

Equity release issues can arise when a homeowner takes out a lifetime mortgage or home reversion plan without being properly informed about the long-term cost, the effect on inheritance, the available alternatives, or whether the product was suitable for their circumstances.

  • ✅ Compound interest not properly explained – Many people are not fully told how quickly the balance can grow over time and how much more may ultimately be owed.
  • ✅ Alternatives not discussed – In some cases, downsizing, remortgaging, family support, or other borrowing options may not have been properly explored before equity release was recommended.
  • ✅ Impact on inheritance not made clear – Some homeowners later discover that their agreement has reduced the value of their estate far more than expected.
  • ✅ Unsuitable advice – Equity release may not have been appropriate for your age, financial position, future plans, or family circumstances.
  • ✅ Early repayment charges or restrictions – Some agreements include fees or terms that become a serious problem if circumstances change.
  • ✅ Lack of clear explanation – If the risks, costs, or long-term consequences were not explained properly, it may be worth reviewing how the agreement was sold.

Your Rights with Equity Release

Across the UK, equity release products should only be recommended where they are suitable, clearly explained, and sold with proper regard to the homeowner’s long-term interests.

  • Receive clear and suitable advice – Any equity release recommendation should be based on your circumstances, needs, and future financial position.
  • Understand the long-term cost – You should be told clearly how interest, charges, and repayment terms could affect the total amount owed over time.
  • Be informed about alternatives – If other options were available, these should have been properly considered before equity release was recommended.

In the UK, these protections may be supported by consumer law, financial conduct standards, and principles of fair advice, including:

  • Financial Conduct Authority principles – Regulated firms must communicate clearly, act fairly, and ensure products are suitable for the customer.
  • Misrepresentation Act 1967 – If the agreement, risks, costs, or likely effect were presented inaccurately, this may strengthen your position.
  • Consumer protection and financial redress routes – Depending on the circumstances, there may be routes to complaint, review, or compensation.
